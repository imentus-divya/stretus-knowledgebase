# 1. Indian Market Foundations

This chapter grounds algorithmic trading in **India’s regulatory and market structure**. It focuses on **equities and equity derivatives** on **NSE** and **BSE**, which cover the majority of retail and prop algo activity.

---

## 1.1 SEBI and the regulatory frame

### Definition

**SEBI** (Securities and Exchange Board of India) is the statutory regulator for securities markets in India. It sets rules for exchanges, clearing corporations, brokers, **algorithmic trading**, short selling, margins, and investor protection.

### Why it matters for algos

- Brokers must implement **risk controls** and audit trails for client algos.
- **Exchange** rules define order types, session timings, circuit limits, and surveillance messages.
- Compliance is **not optional**: your platform should document **who approves algos**, **versioning**, and **kill switches** in line with broker and exchange requirements.

### Practical takeaway

Your algo platform should integrate **broker-provided risk parameters** (margin, quantity limits, throttle limits) and never assume “exchange API = unconstrained trading.”

---

## 1.2 Exchanges: NSE and BSE

### NSE (National Stock Exchange)

- Large share of **cash** and **F&O** volume.
- Key indices: **Nifty 50**, **Bank Nifty**, and many sector/strategy indices.
- Derivatives suite: index and stock **futures & options**.

### BSE (Bombay Stock Exchange)

- **Sensex** and broad listings; significant cash flow; derivatives overlap with NSE for many names.

### Clearing

- **NSCCL** (NSE clearing) and **ICCL** (BSE clearing) handle novation, settlement, and margin collection.
- Your **broker** is the interface to clearing for most algo traders.

### Practical takeaway

Symbol universes, **lot sizes**, and **circuit limits** can differ in detail—always use **exchange-published** contract files and security master data.

## 1.3 Market sessions and phases (typical equity cash)

Timings below are **illustrative**; verify **current** NSE/BSE circulars.

| Phase (conceptual) | Role |
|---------------------|------|
| Pre-open / call auction | Order collection; equilibrium price discovery for open |
| Normal market | Continuous trading |
| Closing | May include call auction close where applicable |

### Algo implications

- **Pre-open** behaviour differs from continuous session: your bar logic must define whether **opening price** comes from auction print or first trade.
- **Closing** auctions affect last-bar semantics for EOD strategies.

---

## 1.4 Circuit filters and trading halts

### Price bands

Stocks trade within **daily price bands** (circuits) set by exchange rules; breaches can trigger **freeze** or **call auction** mechanics depending on segment and rule version.

### Index-wide halts

Market-wide **halts** exist for extreme moves (rules evolve—verify current framework).

### Algo implications

- Backtests must model **“no trade”** or **auction** states, not only continuous limit-order books.
- Risk modules should react to **halt** and **surveillance** flags from data feeds.

---

## 1.5 Surveillance: ASM, GSM, and similar frameworks

### ASM (Additional Surveillance Measure)

Exchange may tag securities under **ASM** with **graded surveillance** (e.g. margin or trade restrictions). Lists are **periodically revised**.

### GSM (Graded Surveillance Measure)

Often associated with **smaller / riskier** names; can imply **forced delivery-only** or other restrictions by stage.

### Algo implications

- Maintain **security attributes** in your master data and **filter** universe for strategies that cannot operate under restricted modes.
- **Liquidity** and **margin** for these names can change abruptly.

---

## 1.6 What “algorithmic trading” means in India (conceptual)

SEBI frameworks distinguish **algorithmic trading** from manual trading for **reporting, controls, and audit**. In practice:

- Orders generated by **automated systems** must be **tagged** and traceable.
- Brokers validate **algo IDs** and may require **approval** for strategy changes.
- **Co-location** and **DMA** (direct market access) are regulated channels used by sophisticated participants.

### Platform design hint

Store **strategy_id**, **version**, **timestamp**, and **human approver** (where required) with order logs for audit replay.

---

## 1.7 Liquidity and participation

### Structure

- **Domestic institutions** (mutual funds, insurers), **FPIs**, **proprietary desks**, **HFT**, and **retail** all interact on the same central limit order book (CLOB) for standard sessions.

### India-specific nuance

- **Concentration** in index names and **Bank Nifty** components often yields **tighter spreads** than mid/small caps.
- **Impact cost** on **illiquid** mid-caps can dominate PnL for size-sensitive algos.

---

## Summary

Indian foundations = **SEBI + exchange + clearing** + **session design** + **surveillance lists** + **circuit behaviour**. Algo platforms must treat these as **first-class data** alongside prices and volumes.
