# RSI overbought / oversold

The **Relative Strength Index (RSI)** measures recent speed and magnitude of price changes. Traders often treat very high RSI as **overbought** and very low RSI as **oversold**, sometimes as mean-reversion or exhaustion cues (especially in range-bound names on NSE).

## Overview

RSI is bounded (commonly 0–100). It does **not** predict direction by itself; it describes **how stretched** recent moves have been.

## Signal definition (common variants)

- **Mean-reversion style**: RSI above **70** (or 80) → look for fade / tighten risk; below **30** (or 20) → look for bounce / reduce short exposure.
- **Trend style**: in strong trends, RSI can remain “overbought” for long periods—so many traders only take RSI signals **with** trend filters.

## Parameters (typical)

| Parameter | Common values |
|-----------|----------------|
| Lookback | 14 bars |
| Overbought | 70 (or 75–80) |
| Oversold | 30 (or 20–25) |

## How to interpret

Use RSI with **context**: volatility regime, liquidity, and whether the stock is **trending** or **choppy**. On illiquid small-caps, prints can spike RSI intraday without durable follow-through.

## Risks and limitations

RSI can diverge from price; false signals are common without filters. Educational content only—not investment advice.
